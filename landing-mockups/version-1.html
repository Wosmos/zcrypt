You bring the storage

Your files sit on your own GitHub, GitLab, or HuggingFace account, encrypted, behind your own token. We never hold your data.

No passphrase recovery — on purpose

We can't reset a passphrase we never receive. That's not a missing feature; it's the proof the encryption is real — a service that could recover your files isn't one that can't read them. Keep your passphrase safe: if you lose it, your files stay encrypted forever, including from us.

FAQ

Questions, answered plainly

No hedging. If a limitation exists, it's stated here.

Can zcrypt see my files?

No. Files are encrypted on your device with AES-256-GCM before anything is uploaded. Your passphrase is turned into a key locally and never sent to us. The server only ever handles ciphertext.

What does "bring your own storage" mean?

You connect your own GitHub, GitLab, or HuggingFace account with your own access token. Your encrypted files are stored there, under your control. zcrypt provides the encryption pipeline, not the disk.

Is it really free? What's the catch?

It's free and open source. The reason it can be free is that you supply the storage — there are no servers full of your files for us to pay for.

What happens if I forget my passphrase?

There's no recovery. Because your passphrase never leaves your device, we have nothing to reset it with. If you lose it, your files remain encrypted permanently — to you and to us. Store it somewhere safe.

How does sharing work without the server seeing the key?

When you share a file (/send) or an encrypted note (/pad), the decryption key is placed in the URL fragment (the part after #). Browsers never transmit the fragment to the server, so the key reaches the recipient but never us — not even in access logs.

Is there a file-size limit?

No. Files are automatically split into 10MB chunks, so the pipeline handles a small note and a very large archive the same way.

Can I use it from the terminal?

Yes. There's a native Go terminal client (TUI) alongside the web app and a desktop app. Same encryption everywhere.

Is it actually open source?

Yes — every line is public. You can read the source, audit the cryptography, and verify the claims on this page yourself.

Why not just call it "zero-knowledge"?

We use "zero-knowledge" to describe the experience — we genuinely can't see your files. Technically, the precise term is client-side (end-to-end) encryption: keys are derived and used on your device and never leave it.
